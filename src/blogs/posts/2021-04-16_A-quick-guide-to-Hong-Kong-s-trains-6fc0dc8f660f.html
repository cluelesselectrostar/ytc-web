Peak Tram

The peak tram is the first mode of rail transport in Hong Kong, running between the Peak, mid levels and city centre. It runs on rails, but in fact it is a ‚Äúfunicular railway‚Äù with cable hauled vehicles.

It‚Äôs main purpose was to serve the elite residing on the Victoria peak, and today it is a tourist attraction in its own right. The track is just around 1.4km long, but it travels nearly 400m in elevation, via the peak‚Äôs mountainous terrain.

The Light Rail (links to an Aussie‚Äôs view of Hong Kong) operates in the Tuen Mun, Yuen Long, and later Tin Shui Wai new towns of New Territories West. Tuen Mun and Tin Shui Wai, in particular, were new towns designed with the light rail system in mind, so space was reserved along main roads.

So here comes the comparisons. Oddly, the DLR commenced in 1987, 1 year prior to Hong Kong‚Äôs LRT. Like the DLR, the LRT runs on (decently heavy) 36 tonne vehicles. Unlike the DLR, the LRT has many road crossings, which makes its operational aspects more similar to tramlink. Unlike tramlink, the LRT requires a greater amount of the platform infrastructure due to the non low-floor design. Similar to the both systems, LRT stops are unmmaned, which makes the system prone to issues like vandalism and fare evasion.

Of all 3 systems, I like tramlink the most. It‚Äôs vehicles are sleek, and operation-wise it makes sense. It is a cleaner alternative for buses, and the platform infrastructure required is minimal due to the trams‚Äô low-floor design. More importantly it feels right in place for a street setting. Hong Kong‚Äôs LRT encounters the same dilema as the DLR, and on top of that, it is considered by many a worse solution than the buses it was meant to replace. Of all 3 systems, I think it is the worst thought-out, as the system occupies quite a bit of road infrastructure, but fails to blend into a street setting gracefully.

A quick comparison on train interiors

Considering that all heavy rail services in Hong Kong are rapid transit style, it‚Äôs natural to compare it to London‚Äôs underground. For those accustomed to travelling on London‚Äôs ‚Äútube‚Äù, you might be slightly surprised to find that Hong Kong‚Äôs trains better resemble something like a TfL rail, overground or LU subsurface (Circle, Metropolitan, District, Hammersmith and City Line) service. The reasons for that are:

  • Hong Kong‚Äôs system itself is not as ancient as London‚Äôs underground, so the infrastructure is designed to accomodate larger trains with a greater passenger capacity.
  • Walkthrough gangways between carriages have been here for quite a while and I‚Äôm not quite sure why this hasn‚Äôt been implemented on the London Underground until the recent S stock.
  • Air conditioning was implemented since electrification. In contrast, only the S stock has air conditioning, and it looks like a big challenge to implement AC in the deep level tube lines, considering the constraints on ventilation and the outdated electrical mains.

All in all, this contributes to a more spacious, comfortable (but arguably ‚Äúclinically feeling‚Äù) look for Hong Kong‚Äôs carriage interiors. Nevertheless this is the trend all train manufacturers are adopting today, for practicality concerns.

One striking similarity you might see is that R-stock has the same hanging hand-straps as all Class 378 and 700. Apparently they are called TOPADSTRAP and are manufactured by an Austrian company called Faigle. The appearance of the hand-straps is subjective, but personally I think they don‚Äôt feel ergonomic. I like the hefty feeling of a conventional metallic hand-strap.
